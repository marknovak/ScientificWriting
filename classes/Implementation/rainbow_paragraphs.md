# Rainbow paragraphs

The "rainbow paragraph" structure is a colour-coded pedagogical tool used to highlight a paragraph's organization. It maps the components of a paragraph to colors, helping to ensure that you have a complete, balanced paragraph.  Paragraphs certainly don't have to rigidly adhere to the structure of a rainbow paragraph (_except for starting with a clear topic sentence!_) and your writing may diverge from it in usefully creative ways, but the structure of a rainbow paragraph is always a good default and a great way to outline a paragraph.

## To do (_before_ class)

Come to class having picked one written paragraph from your _Introduction_, _Results_, or _Discussion_ that you'd like to analyze, and one unwritten paragraph from either for which you've sketched out an outline.  You'll be sharing them with a classmate. (Paragraphs from _Methods_ sections might work too, but they tend to have few colors.)

## The Rainbow Paragraph Structure

- <span style="color: red;">Red</span> (_Topic Sentence_): Introduces the main idea of the paragraph.
- <span style="color: orange;">Orange</span> (_Context_): Provides background information. 
- <span style="color: yellow;">Yellow</span>/<span style="color: green;">Green</span> (_Evidence/Data/Details_): Specific facts, data, or steps in a process.
- <span style="color: blue;">Blue</span> (_Explanation/Analysis_): Explains why or how the evidence supports the topic sentence.
- <span style="color: purple;">Purple</span> (_Conclusion_): Restates the main idea in a new way or summarizes the significance. ￼

You'll find some examples below.  Note that, excepting <span style="color: red;">red</span> and <span style="color: purple;">purple</span>, the same colour can be used for multiple sentences within a paragraph.


## Activity

### Part 1

Exchange your written paragraph with a classmate.  Spend up to 15 minutes colour-coding your classmate's paragraph and then spend up to 10 minutes more colour-coding your own paragraph. (_25 minutes total._)

**_Importantly_: You do _not_ need to use all colours!  If it's not clear how a given sentence should be coloured, don't colour it!**

Give your classmate back their paragraph and compare their colour-coding to yours.  Considering both sets of paragraphs, spend up to 15 minutes to discuss differences and what made sentences easy or difficult to categorize.

### Part 2

Spend up to 10 minutes colour-coding the outline of your own unwritten paragraph, then modify and elaborate on it as desired.  Then explain the content and structure of your paragraph to your classmate (_10 minutes each, for a total of 20 additional minutes_).

## Example 1 (_short version_)

<span style="color: red;">Predator–prey interactions are dynamic ecological relationships in which predator feeding rates depend on prey density and behavioral flexibility.</span>

<span style="color: orange;">In population ecology, these interactions are often described using functional responses, which quantify how a predator’s per capita consumption rate changes as prey abundance varies.</span>

<span style="color: yellow;">Holling’s Type I functional response assumes a linear increase in consumption with prey density, Type II incorporates handling time and produces a saturating curve, and Type III generates a sigmoidal curve due to low-density prey refugia or learning effects.
The Type III response is often also interpreted as encapsulating positive prey-switching which occurs when predators disproportionately target the most abundant prey as relative prey frequencies change.</span>

<span style="color: blue;">The mechanistic differences between functional responses matter because handling constraints limit intake at high prey densities in Type II and III systems, but does not in classical Type I systems.  These differences can modify community composition and potentially dampen or amplify oscillations in models of predator-prey and food web.  For example, frequency-dependent selection in Type III systems can stabilize prey populations by intensifying top-down pressure on dominant prey.</span>

<span style="color: purple;">Understanding predator–prey dynamics therefore requires integrating functional response theory and behavioral processes, like prey switching, to explain how consumption rates scale with prey availability and ultimately structure ecological communities.</span>

## Example 2 (_longer version_)

<span style="color: red;">Predator–prey dynamics in multispecies systems are governed by density-dependent functional responses and adaptive foraging behaviors that jointly determine population stability and community composition.</span>

<span style="color: orange;">In particular, when predators exploit more than one prey species, their per capita consumption rates depend not only on total prey density and environmental context but also on relative prey frequencies and behavioral mechanisms such as prey-switching that influence prey choice.</span>

<span style="color: yellow;">Empirical work across terrestrial and aquatic systems demonstrates that many invertebrate and vertebrate predators exhibit Type II functional responses characterized by a decelerating intake rate as handling time constrains consumption.  Other systems——especially those involving predator learning, ontogenetic diet shifts, or prey refugia——exhibit Type III responses with sigmoidal intake curves and reduced predation at low prey densities.</span>

<span style="color: green;">For example, in experimental mesocosms containing two competing prey species, predators have been observed to exhibit prey-switching behavior by disproportionately attacking the initially more abundant prey prior to measurable shifts in prey relative abundance, resulting in transiently elevated mortality for dominant prey and altered competitive trajectories among prey taxa.</span>

<span style="color: yellow;"> Many other studies have shown parameter estimates of attack rates ($a$) and handling times ($h$) to vary with temperature, habitat complexity, and predator body size, demonstrating strong environmental context dependence in realized feeding rates.</span>

<span style="color: blue;">Theoretical models incorporating these mechanisms predict that Type II responses can generate limit cycles when prey self-limitation rates are low (growth rates are high) and refugia are absent, whereas Type III responses and frequency-dependent predation associated with prey-switching are more likely to dampen oscillations and promote coexistence. Behavioral plasticity can thus modify the stability properties derived from Type II formulations by introducing feedbacks between prey availability and predator foraging decisions.</span>

<span style="color: purple;">Collectively, these findings underscore that understanding predator–prey interactions in natural communities requires integrating functional response forms and adaptive behaviors such as prey-switching to appropriate capture the mechanistic pathways that link individual foraging decisions to population- and community-level dynamics.</span>

## Example 3 (_not on predator-prey interactions!_)

<span style="color: red;">The evolution of genomes is driven by multiple molecular processes that alter genetic material across generations.</span>

<span style="color: orange;">Genomes consist of DNA sequences that encode genes and regulatory elements.  They vary widely in size, structure, and content among species due to short and long-term evolutionary changes.</span>

<span style="color: yellow;">Several mechanisms contribute to genomic evolution, including point mutations that alter individual nucleotides, gene duplication events that create additional copies of genes, horizontal gene transfer in microbes, and large-scale structural changes such as inversions, transpositions, and whole-genome duplications.</span>

<span style="color: green;">Comparative genomics has revealed numerous examples of these processes; for instance, duplicated genes in vertebrates often diverge to acquire new functions, while transposable elements can proliferate within genomes and significantly increase genome size.</span>

<span style="color: blue;">These processes generate genetic variation that natural selection, genetic drift, and recombination can act upon, allowing genomes to evolve new functions, regulatory networks, and levels of biological complexity.</span>

<span style="color: purple;">Thus, genome evolution reflects the cumulative effects of mutation, structural change, and evolutionary forces acting over short and long timescales to shape the diversity of genetic architectures observed across the tree of life.</span>
