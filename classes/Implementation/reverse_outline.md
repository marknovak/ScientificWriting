# Reverse outline

A reverse outline is a revision strategy where you create an outline of a draft after it has already been written. 
Instead of planning what you want to say, you are mapping out what you actually said. 

Reverse outlining will help you diagnose structural problems that are often difficult to see during drafting. 
When writers focus on generating content, paragraphs can drift from the intended argument, mix multiple ideas, or fail to clearly support the paper’s central claim. 
By stepping back and summarizing the function and topic of each paragraph, you can evaluate whether the manuscript’s structure is coherent, logical, and aligned with its goals.
The process typically involves reading through a draft paragraph by paragraph and writing a short description of each paragraph’s purpose in the margin or in a separate document. 
These summaries collectively form a simplified “map” of the manuscript’s structure. 
This map allows you to quickly assess whether ideas progress logically, whether evidence appears in appropriate places, and whether any paragraphs are redundant, misplaced, or missing.

For an individual paragraph, a reverse outline identifies two core things:
1) What it says (_Topic_): The main idea or point being made; its central conceptual content.
2) What it does (_Function_): How the paragraph serves the manuscript; its rhetorical role within the larger argument.

## Example
For the second (longer) example provided in the [rainbow paragraphs](rainbow_paragraphs.md) activity, a reverse outline could look as follows:

#### What it says (_Topic_)

This paragraph explains how predator–prey dynamics in multispecies systems are shaped by functional responses and adaptive behaviors such as prey-switching, and how empirical evidence and theoretical models show that these mechanisms influence population stability, coexistence, and community structure. 
It serves the manuscript by introducing foundational concepts and motivating the importance of functional responses at the level of individual predators for the understanding of predator-prey and food web dynamics.

#### What it does (_Function_)

* **Frames the core ecological mechanisms** governing predator–prey dynamics (functional responses and adaptive foraging).
* **Provides conceptual context** by explaining how prey density, prey frequency, and behavioral processes influence predator consumption rates.
* **Introduces empirical patterns** demonstrating that predators commonly exhibit Type II or Type III functional responses across ecosystems.
* **Presents a concrete experimental example** (mesocosm prey-switching experiments) illustrating how predator behavior affects prey mortality and competition.
* **Adds supporting empirical evidence** showing that key functional-response parameters vary with environmental conditions and predator traits.
* **Connects empirical findings to theory** by explaining how different functional responses and prey-switching influence stability, oscillations, and coexistence in population models.
* **Synthesizes the argument** by concluding that predictive models of predator–prey dynamics must consider functional responses and adaptive foraging behaviors.


## Activity

### Part 1
Produce a reverse outline for each of your paragraphs as objectively as you can.  Don't do any editing or modifying; keep notes if you don't want to forget something. (_up to 30 minutes_)

### Part 2
Use your reverse outline to assess whether the sentences in your paragraphs actually say what you need them to say and do _for the manuscript_. (_up to 15 minutes_)

### Part 3
Pull away from the sentences of the paragraphs to a higher, manuscript level focus and assess how each paragraph as whole serves the manuscript and its audience. (_up to 15 minutes_)
